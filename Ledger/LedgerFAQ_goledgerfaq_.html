Why aren’t my funds added to Square Card?

Moneysweeper sweeps ledger’s cleared funds for Square Card. Funds are not cleared if the merchant is frozen or unsettleable for any reason. Cleared funds refer to payments that have cleared risk suspicions. This distinction is most notable for Square Card and Instant Transfer use cases because only payments that have cleared risk are eligible to be moved to the Card balance or to be transferred out via Instant Transfer.

If the merchant has been reviewed and cleared by ops, check that we’ve properly cleared all associated freeze blockers in ledger.

Check the merchant settlement state via `ListMerchantSettlementStates`. If the merchant has any additional blockers other than `BIZBANK_ACCOUNT`, use `DeleteFreeze` to remove them one by one. For example, a merchant may have been frozen with both a “FROZEN” and “DEBIT_BLOCK” reason, and clearing them requires deleting both individually.

If the merchant has a settlement blocker related to bank accounts (for example, noncompliant return), the merchant will need to unlink and relink their bank account to clear the settlement blocker. Customer service should advise merchants to do this.

Why did the merchant get debited?

Ledger nightly settlement happens in batches. US settlement happens at 8PM PT.

Payments coming in during a certain time window, for example, 5PM from day 0 to 5PM on day 1, get batched together for day 1’s settlement. Payments coming in after 5PM on day 1 will go into day 2’s settlement. A merchant’s deposit schedule affects this time window. For example, consider a merchant with a deposit schedule of 12AM to 12AM due to their business hours. Payments taken between day 1’s 12AM to day 2’s 12AM will be settled on day 2.

Prior to 02 April 2020, non payment entries, like holds, are a different case. They are batched on the default 5PM to 5PM time window from when they are created, regardless of deposit schedule. For example, a hold placed on day 1 at 3PM will be included in day 1’s settlement even if the merchant’s deposit schedule is 12AM to 12AM and payments taken around the same time will go into day 2’s settlement.

So while it can appear that a merchant has sufficient total funds for some hold, it is possible that our batching process splits the available funds, assigns the hold to one, and creates a net negative amount for one night’s settlement. This net negative amount would then mean we debit the merchant’s bank account.

Does Ledger automatically retry failed ACHs?

In a sense, but not directly.  Ledger does not retry the exact same ACH attempt.

Instead, when an ACH fails (debit or credit), ledger will create ledger entries of the type RETURNED_LEDGER_ENTRY for each ledger entry in the ACH batch that failed.  Those ledger entries will be batched like any other ledger entries (and may be combined with new ledger entries).  The next time settlement runs for that merchant, the RETURNED_LEDGER_ENTRY will be settled like other ledger entries.

Typically, if ledger encounters 3 failed bank debits, it will unlink the bank account (resulting in no more settlement).  There are other reasons settlement might be blocked such as a freeze.

Why did my refund get debited when I had a positive balance?

See also: an overview of Ledger’s batching system.

Please note that some behavior may be slightly different due to more recent changes. For example, as of 02 April 2020, non-payment batches should also respect close-of-day hours. This means that a refund should settle at the same time as payments that were captured before the refund.

However, for certain merchants, this behavior may still be happening due to other factors, such as manual transfers, Square Card and/or a custom deposit schedule.

For merchants using manual transfers, they are considered unsettleable but still debitable.  This means we will generally not take money from their Square balance to cover their refunds, and instead will generally go directly to their linked bank account to recover funds.

For an active Square Card merchant, positive funds are transferred from Ledger to Banklin in a process called sweeping by the Square Card team. In Ledger’s point of view, these funds have “settled” as we have already given the funds to the merchant through their choice of deposit mechanism. However, Square Card can reverse sweep funds back into Ledger when a merchant owes Square money since Square Card defers to Ledger to debit the merchant’s bank account when necessary, as is the case with refunds. But because of batching rules and the timing of events, the refund and positive funds from Square Card may not offset each other, causing the merchant to see a bank debit when they appear to have funds to cover it.

For merchants with a custom deposit schedule, the cutoff for a settlement period varies according to their custom schedule. Refunds are eligible to be debited based on the time of the original capture. This often makes refunds eligible to be settled in the very next deposit. However, any incoming payments or other activity will continue to follow standard batching rules, even if they come in roughly around the same time as the refund. Standard batching rules attempt to batch entries into the next available settlement they are eligible for. This is usually the next available settlement if the ledger entry’s effective time is before the settlement period cutoff. With a custom deposit schedule, that cutoff can be earlier or later than expected and force the entry into the next day’s settlement period.

Note that these are not mutually exclusive and can combine.

Why did I get debited after X years? Long overdue activity

Sometimes a seller reactivates their Square account after a long period of inactivity. It’s possible that any lingering credits or debits get applied as soon as they are reactivated, causing unexpected bank activity in the seller’s account.

This category of issues may have different answers for different questions. If the seller closed their account, their debt or remaining credits may have been settled through other means. There should be a corresponding entry in Ledger, but it may not have been recorded properly. It’s also possible the accounting history did not start in ledger but was later translated over. For example, from sqweb to ledger. However, not all of the history may have been ported over - like how and why money got held or released.

When ledger’s information is fragmented, it’s time to check with other people and other teams, such as recovery and possibly legal for next steps. If we want to adjust a merchant’s balance, the customer service rep can do it via Regulator. This is preferable because the Regulator action calls sqweb, which then flows into Ledger.

What does “merchant” in Ledger mean? Are they Merchants or Units?

Ledger has a concept of “merchants” but they are really “accounts”. These are accounts in which ledger entries are associated (payments, refunds, etc). Ledger doesn’t actually differentiate between Merchant and Unit, but we shard our database by accounts. Payments are made on the unit level, so their account/merchant is associated with the unit token. However, subscriptions are on the merchant level, so in ledger, they are associated with the Merchant token. This is why most activity seen in Ledger is on the unit level, with the exception being subscriptions.

Why don’t I see Japanese merchants being debited? Why aren’t Japanese refunds being settled?

Japanese bank accounts are credit only, so we do not debit them. When Japanese merchants have a negative balance, Ledger does not settle them. We rely on new credits coming in to offset the merchant’s balance. If the JP merchant doesn't have sufficient balance to cover their debit, it goes into an extremely manual process outside of Ledger.

I expected the refund to result in a debit! I don’t see an Inbound Transfer (or debit) for this refund.

Refunds and how they settle are influenced by a number of things, as there are different types of transfers.

Not all transfers are credits and debits to a merchant’s bank account. Outbound Transfers can include ACH credits, instant deposits, manual deposits, and net zero transfers (also called 0 amount transfers). Inbound Transfers can include ACH debits and Pay-Ins. Ledger is not responsible for all of these transfer types. Ledger drives ACH credits and debits, instant deposits, and net zero transfers. Even then, instant deposits is not owned by Accounting Engineering but the Balance and Transfers team, and net zero transfers may indicate a transfer from Ledger to another service for a different product.

Refunds with Manual Transfers

It’s important to note that Square’s external facing documentation is simplified and does not cover 100% of specific edge cases.  The current actual behavior is complicated.

Merchants on Manual Transfers have their funds accumulate in Ledger until such time when they initiate a deposit. Ledger entries stay unsettled until then.  While these Ledger entries accumulate, they continue to undergo the normal batching process described here.  This means that if you have a payment credit in Ledger, and a refund debit, the amount withdrawn from your externally linked bank account is not necessarily the entire refund amount; it may be that the refund is netted with the pre-existing credit.  This allows Square to recover some of the debit funds without needing to withdraw the entire amount from an external account.

When a manual transfer is initiated, ledger entries will become settled as funds are then transferred, either through nightly settlement or through Instant Transfers as chosen by the merchant.

Manual deposits is not owned by Accounting Engineering so further questions should be directed to the Balance and Transfers team.

Refunds with Square Card

It’s important to note that Square’s external facing documentation is simplified and does not cover 100% of specific edge cases.  The current actual behavior is complicated.

Merchants on Square Card have their available balance transferred from Ledger to Banklin (“sweeping”). However, Ledger remains responsible for collecting merchant debts owed to Square.  In the case of seller debt, including but not limited to a refund, funds from Square Card are transferred from Banklin to Ledger (“reverse sweeping”).  Further external bank transfers will occur to collect any further debt amounts, which may or may not be the exact amount of a specific refund.

Square Card is not owned by Accounting Engineering so further questions related to Square Card activity should be directed to the Square Card (aka business banking) team.

What is effective time?

Note that created time and effective time are also referred to as “created at” time and “effective at” times.

Ledger entries have two types of timestamps: created time and effective time. Created time is self-explanatory - they are when the ledger entries are created. Effective time is when the ledger entry can be applied to a merchant’s current available balance.

The effective time is used for settlement: the daily batch a ledger entry goes into is determined by its effective time and the merchant’s deposit schedule, and a batch is eligible to be credited to (or debited from) the merchant only if its effective time is before the nightly settlement run.

For some ledger entry types, such as return ledger entries and disputes, the effective time is simply the created time,  For others, such as cost plus and capital, the client sets the value. For refunds, the effective time is set to that of the original capture; for captures, the effective at is set to the current time plus some risk review lag.

Thus, a rejected refund may come after Ledger has already tried to take money from the merchant.

What does it mean when a merchant is unsettleable? Or undebitable?

When a merchant is unsettleable, Ledger will not settle any credit (positive) batches the merchant has. When a merchant is undebitable, Ledger will not settle any debit (negative) batches the merchant has. They are basically opposite terms to a merchant being creditable or debitable. (Note that settleable is also used synonymously with creditable.)

There are many factors that affect merchant settleability. The usual are the risk factors, which include the frozen and debit_blocked states as described in this overview. As a recap, being frozen means a merchant is not creditable but still debitable, or in other words, a merchant is unsettleable. Being debit blocked means a merchant is both not creditable and not debitable, or in other words, a merchant is both unsettleable and undebitable.

If a merchant does not have a verified bank account linked, Ledger would not be able to settle them. This merchant would be both unsettleable and undebitable.

If a merchant has a verified bank account linked that is said to be credit only, then that means the merchant’s bank account only allows money transfers into the bank account. Thus Ledger would only be able to credit the merchant. This merchant is creditable but undebitable.

What does it mean when a merchant is unsettleable due to BIZBANK_ACCOUNT?

If a merchant is on Square Card or manual deposits, Ledger has a blocker called BIZBANK_ACCOUNT for them. For credit settlement, the merchant’s money is moved from Ledger to another deposit mechanism of choice (for example, Square Card). However, the Square Card and manual deposit services do not have the functionality to pull money from the merchant if they owe Square money, so they rely on Ledger to do any debiting. Thus the merchant in Ledger is debitable but unsettleable.

What is Ledger batching?

The basic overview of batching can be found here.

What is a ledger batch?

A batch is a group of ledger entries grouped by types and time. A batch can only contain entries within a certain settlement period (typically one day) with start and end times depending on the merchant’s close of day. Within a single settlement period, a merchant can have multiple batches, even those of the same type.  Batches are the primary unit of what gets transferred when the settlement pipelines run.

How does it work?

Ledger generally has two types of batches, payments and non-payments. Both types can be either creditable or debitable.

When a ledger entry is created, Ledger groups it together with other ledger entries associated with the same payment before determining the batch to which it belongs, so these are generally payment type batches. For ledger entries that don’t have an associated payment, these may form their own single-element group or have other grouping rules to follow. For example, holds and releases generally batch together. These batches have non-payment batch types. To determine whether a group should be in the settleable or debitable batch, the net amount of the group is used: if it is positive, all ledger entries in the group are placed in the settleable batch; otherwise, they are all placed in the debitable batch.

Why does this classification matter to us?  The settleability and debitability of a seller are two independent states that determine which batches can be transferred during the nightly pipeline: settleable batches can only be transferred if the seller is settleable, and debitable batches can only be transferred if the seller is debitable.  There are various reasons why a seller may not be settleable or debitable; for example, they may be frozen (not settleable but still debitable), their bank account may only allow deposits (not debitable but still settleable), or they may not have a verified bank account linked (neither debitable nor settleable).  As another example, sellers that use manual transfer, including Square Card sellers, are not settleable, but remain debitable.

For example, let’s say we have a merchant that has these 4 batches at settlement time

  • Payment_settleable PS
  • Payment_debitable PD
  • Nonpayment_settleable NPS
  • Nonpayment_debitable NPD

If the merchant is also frozen, then PD and NPD will be settled.

Or if the merchant is debit blocked, then none of these batches will be settled.

Or if the merchant is on Square Card, then PD and NPD will be settled.

Note that merchant settlement states can combine. For example, if the above example merchant is on Square Card but has a credit only bank account linked, then none of the 4 batches above will settle. This is because the Square Card state blocks all settleable batches (PS and NPS) and the credit only bank account blocks all debitable batches (PD and NPD).

Can I guarantee that a merchant won’t be debited from their bank account when they have available funds?

No. Currently, there is no way to guarantee that debits are made from the merchant’s available balance rather than their linked bank account. A large number of factors can play into this, including Ledger’s batching system, the merchant’s settlement state, whether they are on Square Card or manual deposits, the effective times of the ledger entries in question, and the time of the settlement pipeline.

The following is a general guideline:

  • Is the merchant a Square Card or manual deposit seller?
  • Ledger will never net settle their creditable and debitable batches together. The merchant in this state is unsettleable but debitable.
  • (2/2/21) Update: While it remains true that manual deposit merchants are unsettleable but debitable, there is a process in the bankc service that does attempt to net settle their creditable and debitable batches before settlement time through the AutoFoldBalanceEventProcessor. The aforementioned process should net settle creditable and debitable batches once a day starting 15 minutes after a country’s default end of day. This process takes up to 20 minutes for all manual merchants to be net settled. Square Card sellers do not have their balances automatically folded.
  • Does the merchant have available funds in their Square Card?
  • Does the merchant have any settlement blockers? (Such as being frozen or debit blocked)
  • If yes, the settlement blocker may block fund movements so their available funds in their Square Card may not be able to transfer into Ledger.
  • If no, the funds may transfer from Square Card to Ledger. However, this does not guarantee that the amounts net together, as timing and other factors may still apply
  • Or else, does the merchant have a custom close of day?
  • Ledger may net settle their creditable and debitable batches. Please wait a day after the adjustment (including refunds and holds) is made to be on the safe side, due to the possible effective at time of the adjustment.
  • Otherwise, the creditable and debitable batches should transfer together.

Why are there multiple deposits over the weekend?

This is related to a merchant’s deposit schedule, which is also called their (custom) close of day, and Square’s settlement schedule. Deposit transmissions are created by levering the merchant’s deposit schedule. All payments and other activity are batched internally in Ledger based on their deposit schedule. For more information about batching, please go here.

In general, the Ledger batch intervals are based on the merchant’s deposit schedule’s deposit intervals. For example, a merchant with a deposit schedule defined for every day of the week would have batches created for each day. The settlement pipeline creates deposit entities based on these batches. So if the batches eligible for settlement include batches with multiple batch intervals, the pipeline will create separate deposit entities for each of the batch intervals. So although the pipeline ran once, if it created separate deposit entities for a merchant, that merchant will see multiple deposits in their bank account.

The default deposit schedule is configured in Ledger per country, but is generally defined as Monday through Friday (AU) or Monday through Friday plus Sunday (US, CA). For a Monday through Friday deposit schedule, this means that there are 5 batch intervals created during the week that payments and other activity would fall into. In terms of the weekend, payments and other activities that are effective during the Saturday and Sunday intervals would be merged in with the Monday batch interval. Thus the merchant would receive all eligible Saturday, Sunday, and Monday funds on Monday in a single deposit. For a Monday through Friday plus Sunday deposit schedule, there are 6 batch intervals during the week that payments and activities would fall into. For the weekend, Saturday and Sunday payments and other activities would be merged together for the Sunday batch interval.

When a merchant sets a custom close of day, they usually set a close of day for each day of the week. This creates separate batch intervals for each day. This results in what users see as multiple batches over the weekend. For example, a merchant has a close of day set for every day, Monday through Sunday. By Sunday night, the weekend batches have 2 batch intervals: for Saturday and for Sunday. But because Square does not send any settlement files on Saturday, the Saturday batches can only be settled on the next settlement time. In the US and CA, that would be during the Sunday settlement. So Sunday would include both Saturday and Sunday batch intervals and be sent as two separate deposits.

Note that US merchants are on a schedule where Friday settlements are optional, which means that unless the merchant has enabled Friday settlements, their Friday settlements end up pushed back to the next settlement time, which is Sunday. This means that these merchants would see 3 separate deposits on Sunday: one for the Friday batch interval, one for Saturday’s batch interval, and one for Sunday’s batch interval.

In AU where Square only settles Monday through Friday, this means that a merchant on a custom deposit schedule with deposit intervals defined per day would see 3 deposits for their Monday settlement. These 3 deposits account for the Saturday batch interval, the Sunday batch interval, and the Monday batch interval.

Note that JP merchants do not use the ledger batching system and so this does not apply to them.

Square Card, manual deposit users. What are they and what is the difference?

Square Card and manual deposits (also called manual transfers) are two different products. Both Square Card and manual deposit users are represented the same way in ledger. They have a merchant settlement blocker called BIZBANK_ACCOUNT. This acts like a FREEZE such that ledger may only automatically settle the merchant’s negative entries but will not settle their positive entries.

Square Card users are merchants who have enabled business banking to access their Square balance through a debit card. Internally, the funds are moved from the merchant’s ledger balance to their Square Card balance (which is backed by banklin). This process is called sweeping, and normally runs every few minutes. An edge case is when the merchant does not have any cleared funds (such as the merchant being unsettleable or just has $0 cleared balance in ledger), which pushes the sweep job to every 1.5 hours instead.

The Square Card team (#square-debit-card) owns this product so any questions about the Square Card balance or process should be directed to them.

Manual deposit (or manual transfer) users are merchants who have elected out of automatic transfers. This means that rather than having Square (aka ledger) make a deposit to their bank account every day, the merchant wants to store their balance with Square and initiate a transfer manually on their own schedule. The merchant’s funds still live in ledger in this case.

The manual transfer product is owned by the Balance and Transfers (BaT) team (#balance-and-transfers) so any questions about manual transfer settings or status should be directed to them.

How can I differentiate them?

The ledger merchant settlement state BIZBANK_ACCOUNT is visible in regulator’s transfer sections as “manual” (as these were updated for manual transfers).

Since both Square Card and manual transfer users use the same settlement blocker (BIZBANK_ACCOUNT), we can’t tell which product the merchant is using from this blocker status only. Fortunately, there are other ways to check.

In regulator, active Square Card users should have a green pill indicator near the top of the page.

Also, you can check the Square Card section in regulator to see if their card is enabled or not. The Square Card section can be found by clicking “More” and selecting “Square Card”. Or update the user’s regulator URL to the following: https://regulator.sqprod.co/n/users/<UNITTOKEN>?section=sq-card

So merchants with the Manual label and an enabled Square Card indicates a Square Card user. And merchants with the Manual label with an inactive Square Card indicates a manual transfer user.

For those with access to ledger’s RPCs, another way to check for manual transfer status is to call Deposits.GetDepositSchedule and look for paused_settlement in the result.

How do I track down a merchant’s transfer? How do I find a transfer?

A merchant called in saying their bank had a transfer (credit, debit) that they can’t track down.

Through Regulator

If you know the merchant/unit token, you can start from their Regulator page. You can go to their Regulator page through go/m/<merchant_token> or

https://regulator.sqprod.co/n/users/<merchant_token>?section=transfers

Credit transfers should be listed under “Outbound Transfers” while debits are under “Inbound Transfers”. All Ledger-initiated transfers can be found here, but not all listed transfers are Ledger transfers.

If the transfer you are looking for is not in either Outbound or Inbound Transfers, you can also check the Merchant level transfers. (Remember that payments are made on the merchant/unit level so general transfers such as automatic transfers and instant transfers are also on the unit level).

The Merchant level account can be found by going to the unit’s main account. All transfers on the Merchant/main level are likely to be a subscription bill.


If the transfer in question was made before roughly July 2022, you can check if main account’s ledger transfers in the Transfers section at

https://regulator.sqprod.co/n/merchants/<main_token>?section=transfers

If the transfer in question was made more recently, you can go to the Billing section to check if there was a bill for such and such amount during such and such time period. These won’t show up in the Transfers section because subscriptions has shifted off from Ledger-based transfers to Teller ones.

https://regulator.sqprod.co/n/merchants/<main_token>?section=billing-bills

You can also look at the Subscription section for details about the Merchant’s subscriptions and payment methods.

https://regulator.sqprod.co/n/merchants/<main_token>?section=subscriptions

Any further questions about subscription transfers can be directed to the owning team at #saas-help.

By Customer Id

If you have the customer id (also known as end to end ids), you can use Snowflake to find which merchant/unit the transfer is associated with. The customer id is an alphanumeric string that starts with L. These denote transfers initiated by Ledger. Customer ids that start with T are transfers initiated by Teller, which is owned by the #banking-connections team and requires a different way to investigate.

Once you have the Ledger customer ids, go to Snowflake with go/snowflake. Add the customer ids into the following query (replacing the L<ID>s) and run it. You can use the merchant_token to look up the seller’s account that the transfer is for in Regulator.

select * from ledger.raw_feeds.bank_settlement_entry where customerid in ('L<ID>','L<ID>');

And you can use the token to get the specific transfer in Regulator by following this url convention:

https://regulator.sqprod.co/n/users/<merchant_token>/transfers/<token>

Note that sometimes there are customer ids in a different format. For example, Japanese customer ids are composed entirely of numbers. In this case, there are no signs to indicate whether it is a Ledger or Teller transfer.

By other factors (trace number, amount, dates)

Even if you do not have the customer id, you can also use Snowflake to directly query for Ledger transfers with uncertain values. For example, if you don’t know the merchant, but only have a trace number or the amount and date range, you can try to look it up from the Snowflake data. Remember that the Snowflake data is only replicated once a day so more recent transfers may not show up.

Why can’t a merchant see what ledger entries are in a transfer?

This is likely due to the merchant using a settlement product other than automatic transfers.

There are multiple representations of transfers a seller may see on their dashboard depending on the transfer product they use. These include automatic transfers, manual transfers, instant transfers, and Square Card. At their core, the base representation of payments (e.g. captures, refunds) for settlement happens in the Ledger service and are represented as ledger entries. Ledger also drives automatic transfers so it is possible to represent this as “a collection of ledger entries settled in this transfer”. This makes it possible for the seller to see in their transfer dashboard what payments were included in a specific transfer.

However, this only applies to automatic transfers! Manual transfers, instant transfers, and Square Card are all products built off of ledger entries but are not settled by Ledger, and so are represented within Ledger in different ways but basically sum up to “settled via internal transfer to another Square system”. To the seller and internal services like Regulator, this can be confusing since transfer reporting is based almost exclusively on the ledger automatic transfer system.

As Squares, we have access to Regulator, and can see the 2 transfers that make up an actual manual transfer. One of the two transfers is a 0-amount transfer that represents the Ledger internal transfer and contains the ledger entries that make up that manual transfer as well as the accounting ledger entries to record the manual transfer and any remaining balance. The other transfer represents the actual manual transfer sent through the bank networks via Teller, and this is shown in the dashboard UI. The Ledger internal transfer is not directly shown in the dashboard so it can be difficult for the seller to view the corresponding ledger entries settled in the transfer.

How can a merchant trace manual transfers? How can a merchant view which payments end up in which transfer?

A seller can view these ledger entries and the Ledger internal 0-amount transfer as the 0-amount transfers are included in the CSV export of the seller’s transfer report.

Exporting the CSV

  • Go to the seller dashboard → balance → transfers.
  • Adjust the date range to include the dates of the manual transfers around the interested time period. A buffer may be needed on either side.
  • Click on the export button to download the CSV file.
  • (Optional) Import the CSV into Google Docs to make filtering and/or searching easier.

Finding Ledger entries included in a specific manual transfer

  • In the dashboard transfers UI, find the manual transfer you are interested in.
  • Click open and in the opened view, copy the Transfer # field.
  • Open the exported CSV file and search for the Transfer # field.
    For a manual transfer, there should be a single line that contains the desired deposited amount. There should be a similar looking row nearby for the same     Deposit Date and with a negated deposited amount. For example, if the manual transfer was for $10 on 1/1, look for a similar row nearby for -$10 on 1/1.
  • Copy the Deposit ID of this row. This Deposit ID is the Transfer # of the 0-amount internal transfer.
  • Find all rows in the CSV with this copied Deposit ID. These rows are the ledger entries included in the manual transfer.

You can also attempt the other way to find the specific manual transfer that a ledger entry was settled with.

Finding a specific transfer for a given ledger entry

  • Look for the row in the csv file that would correspond to the ledger entry you want to trace.
    The bare minimum would be to look for the row with the correct date and type of ledger entry. For a capture or refund, you can also use the payment token to search the CSV.
  • Copy the Deposit ID from the found row.
    This is the transfer that the ledger entry was settled in, but can represent an actual transfer to/from their deposit mechanism of choice or an internal transfer.
  • Search for all rows with the copied Deposit ID.
  • If there is only 1 row with a Payout type, a Collected amount of 0, and a Fees amount of 0, this is likely a manual transfer to the seller’s bank account.
  • If there are multiple rows, check the sum of the Deposited column.
  • If the sum is nonzero, this is likely to be an automatic transfer to the seller’s bank account or an instant transfer to their deposit mechanism of choice.
  • If the sum is 0, this is an internal transfer.
  • For an internal transfer, check the types of rows in it.
  • Look at the Adjustment type rows in the internal transfer with a negative amount. Take note of the Deposited amount (DA), the Deposit Date (DD), and the Deposit ID.
  • Search for any rows in the CSV with a Deposited amount of -DA (the deposit amount with the opposite sign), with a Payment Date equal to DD, and with a different Deposit ID value from the Adjustment row.
  • If there are none, then this internal transfer can either represent a basic net settling of the seller’s account but no real money movement or represent an internal transfer to a Square Card balance.
  • If there are rows matching this criteria, copy the corresponding Deposit IDs. These Deposit IDs represent other transfers in the chain.

It’s possible that a ledger entry ends up being internally transferred multiple times. For example, if the seller is on manual transfers and does not initiate a transfer over multiple days. In this case, the above steps would need to be done multiple times.
